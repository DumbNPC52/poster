PROSPERO’S ISLAND

THE ARCHITECTURE OF COLONIAL DOMINATION

"I must obey: his art is of such power,
It would control my dam's god, Setebos,
And make a vassal of him." (1.2.373-375)

In this scene, Prospero uses his magic to strip away Caliban’s independence. By forcing a native god like Setebos into the role of a 'vassal' or servant, he shows the true nature of Imperialism: it is the complete takeover of not just someone's land, but also their spiritual identity and their body

"If thou more murmur'st, I will rend an oak
And peg thee in his knotty entrails till
Thou hast howl'd away twelve winters." (1.2.294-296)

Prospero maintains control here through the constant threat of physical pain. These 'tempestuous' threats remind Ariel that his freedom isn't actually real, it's conditional. This perfectly mirrors the unfair power dynamic where a colonial master uses past trauma to keep a servant obedient.

POETIC STRUCTURE & AUTHORITY

Prospero’s commands often use Iambic Pentameter (ten-syllable lines) to establish his formal, royal authority. For example: "I have done nothing but in care of thee" (1.2.16). Shakespeare uses the 'tempest' as a metaphor for the chaos and loss of control felt by those living under Prospero’s strict rule. The stormy language reflects the emotional turmoil of characters like Caliban, who are trapped in a system of oppression and unable to escape the reach of his magic.
