PROSPERO’S ISLAND

THE ARCHITECTURE OF COLONIAL DOMINATION

"I must obey: his art is of such power,
It would control my dam's god, Setebos,
And make him a servant." (1.2.373-375)

In this part, Prospero uses his magic to take away Caliban’s freedom. By forcing a native god like Setebos a 'vassal' or servant, he shows what Imperialism really means: taking over not only someone's land, but also their spiritual identity and their body.

"And I will rend an oak, shouldst thou faintly murmur more.
And tie him by his knotty entrails till.
Thou hast snarl'd away twelve winters. " (1.2.294-296)

The threat of physical pain keeps Prospero in control here. These threats of tempestuousness serve to remind Ariel that his freedom is not real, but an illusion. This reflects the oppressive nature of power where a colonial master exploits his subjects through trauma to keep them on a 'leash'.

How Prospero Uses Language to Rule

The orders of Prospero frequently adopts Iambic Pentameter (ten-syllable lines) to establish his authority and control over the island. He says, "I have done nothing but take care care of thee" (1.2.16). The tempest is a Shakespearean metaphor for the disorder and loss of power experienced by people whose lives were dominated by Prospero's harsh rule. The violent language reveals the characters' psychological struggles, as Caliban, trapped in a system of subjugation, cannot remain beyond Prospero's magic.
